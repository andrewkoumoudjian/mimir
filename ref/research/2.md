## The strongest unified platform

The best concept is a **dual-lens transaction control tower**. I would name it something like **DualLens**, **FinTwin**, or **LedgerLens**. The core idea is simple: every transaction can enter the platform once, be normalised once, and then power three tightly related surfaces.

The first surface is the **Spend Lens** for the Brim side: natural-language analytics, saved charts, spend drivers, merchant and geography breakdowns, FX and cross-border summaries, approval workflows, and policy violations. The second is the **Trust Lens** for the Valsoft side: explainable fraud scoring, linked-entity analysis across cards/devices/IPs/merchants, analyst case workbenches, and alert triage. The third is the **Reporting Lens**: FATCA/CRS self-certification and reasonableness checks, plus FINTRAC ŌĆ£preflightŌĆØ report packs that are schema-valid and audit-ready without actually being submitted. That makes the platform feel like a real card-programme operating system rather than a themed dashboard. ## Practical implementation and compliance design

Begin by creating a **canonical transaction model** that normalises both feeds. You do not need a giant warehouse. You need one clean semantic layer. The important fields are: `source_system`, `source_txn_id`, `event_ts`, `posting_ts`, `amount`, `direction`, `merchant_name_raw`, `merchant_name_norm`, `merchant_category`, `mcc`, `merchant_city`, `merchant_country`, `card_id`, `channel`, `device_id`, `ip_address`, `cardholder_country`, `fx_rate`, `description`, and a small set of derived fields such as `is_cross_border`, `is_card_present_proxy`, `rolling_1h_count`, `rolling_24h_amount`, `new_merchant_for_card`, and `cards_per_device`. The single most underrated deliverable in the whole project is good merchant normalisation, because it improves both the Brim analytics and the Valsoft graph features.

For the **Brim lens**, the winning pattern is a deterministic insight engine plus an explanatory assistant. Derive spend views by merchant, country, category, posting date vs transaction date, debit vs credit, and FX conversion behaviour. Generate saved visuals, monthly spend narratives, merchant concentration, duplicate subscription hints, policy-violation summaries, and approval recommendations. Use the LLM to decide *which* view the user wants and to explain the result, but have deterministic code perform all grouping, filtering, and aggregation. That pattern is supported both by the winning Brim examples and by the safer JSON-first visualisation projects such as Chat2Plot and OpenVizAI.
